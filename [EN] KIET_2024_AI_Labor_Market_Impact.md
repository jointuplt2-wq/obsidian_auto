## Summary

### Research Objectives and Necessity

Artificial intelligence is expected to provide economic growth opportunities, but strategies to enhance competitiveness must account for AI-induced changes in labor demand.
- The economic spillover effects and labor substitution effects of AI are expected to vary by occupation and industry. Therefore, strategies must be developed to minimize job losses while enhancing competitiveness.
- Understanding the actual state of AI adoption by domestic firms and AI-induced changes in labor demand by industry and occupation must precede appropriate policy responses.
- Empirical analysis of domestic labor market changes is insufficient, as prior research results coexist showing both that AI creates jobs through productivity gains and that it reduces labor demand through workforce substitution.

**Research Purpose**: To empirically analyze AI's impact on domestic labor markets by occupation and industry, evaluate current digital talent development policies based on findings, and derive policy recommendations for workforce development and employment policies in preparation for expanded AI utilization.

---

### AI and Employment: Prior Research & Domestic/International Policy Trends

- Research on AI and labor markets has focused primarily on calculating Exposure Indices representing AI's potential for labor substitution, and empirical analysis of employment and wage impacts.

**Major Country Policy Approaches**:
- **United States**: Supports private-sector-led talent development, facilitates visa resolution for attracting high-skilled foreign talent, supports interdisciplinary collaboration education, and expands STEM investment to broaden the talent pool.
- **EU**: Advances high-level talent development through dual master's degree programs, supports researchers by career stage (early/mid/senior) with research grants, and supports business opportunities and patent applications.
- **China**: Pursues simultaneous development of elite talent and industry-convergence talent through AI curriculum establishment and specialized talent programs as a national strategy. Enhances researchers' innovation capabilities through incentive support and research budget autonomy.

**Domestic Policy**:
- Domestic AI talent development policies pursue comprehensive development of specialized talent through university-centered education and general education programs to strengthen current workforce and digital literacy.

### Analysis of AI Adoption's Employment Impact on Domestic Firms

**Data**: Enterprise Activity Survey (기업활동조사), 2017–2021

**Key Findings – AI Adoption Rate**:
- Firms utilizing AI account for less than **4%** of surveyed companies. Information and communication and financial/insurance industries show relatively higher adoption rates.
- The rate of firms that adopted AI and continued using it was only **0.5%**.
- Firms with AI adoption experience are larger in employment, revenue, and profit, and have a higher proportion belonging to large corporate groups.

**DID Analysis Results – Employment**:

| Analysis Type | Result |
|---------------|--------|
| Standard DID – employee count | No significant employment change from AI adoption |
| By industry / by revenue size | No significant employment effect |
| With time lag | No significant employment effect |
| Multi-period DID (basic) | ~3–4% employment increase estimated, but not robust |
| Multi-period DID + firm attributes | Employment increase effect disappears |
| Impact on revenue | No significant revenue increase from AI adoption |

**Interpretation**:
- Standard DID model finds no significant employment change attributable to AI adoption.
- Multi-period DID finds approximately 4% employment increase, but this becomes insignificant when controlling for industry and revenue size.
- Revenue and profit improvement effects are also not found, suggesting no subsequent employment-inducing effects from business performance improvement.

**Data Limitations**:
- Survey period is short (2017–2021) and the number of AI-adopting firms is very small, which may have led to statistically insignificant results.
- If long-term large-scale data on AI adoption is secured, subsequent research can verify the long-term effects of AI adoption.

### Analysis of AI's Impact on Labor Demand

**Method**: Applied Webb (2019) AI Exposure Index (linked AI patent information with occupational task content)

**AI Exposure Index by Occupation (Major Categories)**

| Occupation | AI | Software | Robot |
|------------|-----|----------|-------|
| Managers | 0.497 | 0.411 | 0.204 |
| Professionals and related workers | 0.475 | 0.412 | 0.235 |
| Clerical workers | 0.279 | 0.346 | 0.196 |
| Service workers | 0.281 | 0.354 | 0.453 |
| Sales workers | 0.175 | 0.286 | 0.237 |
| Skilled agricultural workers | 0.534 | 0.724 | 1.253 |
| Craft workers | 0.374 | 0.453 | 0.824 |
| Machine operators | 0.538 | 0.720 | 1.083 |
| Elementary workers | 0.323 | 0.550 | 1.206 |

**Industries with High AI-Exposed Job Ratios (75th percentile, 2022)**:
- Highest ratio: M. Professional, scientific and technical services (44.9%), J. Information and communication (40.5%)
- Most at-risk jobs in absolute numbers: C. Manufacturing

**Top 5 Occupations Most Vulnerable to AI** (by exposure index):
1. Water/recycling machinery operators
2. Construction/electrical/production managers
3. Engineering professionals and technicians
4. Science professionals and related workers
5. Wood/printing/other machinery operators

**Key Analysis Results**:

| Dependent Variable | AI Effect | Software Effect | Robot Effect |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Total employment (basic) | −0.43%* | −0.79%*** | −0.90%*** |
| Total employment + job routineness | Not significant | −0.52%** | −0.64%*** |
| Hiring plans | Not significant | Not significant | −0.49%** |
| Overall job openings | +0.62%*** | Not significant | −0.43%** |
| Job openings: bachelor's–master's | +1.17%*** | Not significant | Not significant |
| Job openings: doctoral | +1.58%*** | Not significant | −1.15%** |

**Important Interpretation**:
- AI's apparent employment reduction effect is largely attributable to **routine tasks** that AI can perform. When controlling for task routineness, AI itself does not show significant employment reduction.
- This contrasts with software and robots, where both task routineness and exposure index show employment reduction.
- Occupations more exposed to AI show **increased demand for bachelor's–doctoral level workers**, suggesting AI complements high-skilled labor while substituting routine tasks.

**R&D Activity Analysis**:
- In industries with active AI R&D (measured by AI patent applications), employment significantly **increases** (coefficient: 1.39% per unit increase in AI score).
- AI and software R&D central industry is both J. Information and communication, but AI requires high-skilled experts (doctoral level), while software shows relatively higher demand for mid-level skills (bachelor's–master's).

### AI Talent Development Status and Policy Tasks

Empirical analysis results and policy review derive the following four policy tasks:

**① Differentiate SW/AI talent and implement AI-specific talent development policy**
- Empirical analysis shows that AI and software development require workers with different skill levels: AI demands relatively high-skilled master's/doctoral talent, while software requires mid-level skills.
- Current digital talent development policies use AI and software concepts interchangeably. SW-centered universities and AI graduate schools need an integrated curriculum system for bachelor's–master's–doctoral levels.
- AI talent development policy goals should be clearly set for high-skilled specialist development. AI graduate school support program evaluation metrics need improvement (add metrics like "top journal papers" to assess talent excellence).

**② Strengthen AI competitiveness through private-sector-led AI innovation**
- Korea's AI competitiveness is assessed at world level 6, but government strategy (6th) is much stronger than private investment (18th).
- Korea's 2013–2022 cumulative private investment was $5.57 billion, much smaller than leading nations (US: $248.9 billion, China: $95.1 billion).
- AI innovation and talent development is currently government-led; private sector mainly participates in government policies.
- **Policy Direction**: Transition practical worker training courses (K-training, Innovation Academy) to be company-led; focus government on basic education (AI graduate schools); provide incentives for private investment to lead AI innovation.

**③ Proactively address barriers to attracting foreign talent**
- Foreign talent utilization can quickly secure high-skilled workers and enhance competitiveness, but current policies are insufficient.
- Leading AI nations (US, China) are actively attracting foreign talent: US expanded STEM visa requirements; 83% of AI doctoral graduates remain in the US after their degree.
- Korea's foreign talent exchange target is only 50 people in core technology fields, and visa period extensions (1→2 years) have limited effectiveness.
- **Policy Direction**: Identify and remove barriers to foreign talent attraction (language issues, compensation constraints, visa problems) and proactively incorporate this into policy.

**④ Proactive response to AI job displacement**
- AI's employment effects have not yet become visible (AI adoption rate is only ~3%, concentrated in large enterprises).
- However, AI is expected to displace labor in the long term. Goldman Sachs (2023) forecasts approximately 300 million full-time jobs will disappear globally.
- Unlike robots (routine manual tasks) and software (routine cognitive tasks), AI can displace **non-routine cognitive tasks**, including managers and professionals—a broader range than previous technologies.
- **Policy Direction**: 
  - Prepare employment countermeasures before AI's displacement effects become visible
  - Forecast jobs that will disappear and emerge; develop talent with required skills
  - Enhance value of jobs resistant to automation (service, healthcare, education)
  - Explore ways to expand employment through productivity effects of AI adoption

## Chapter 1: Introduction

### 1. Research Necessity and Background

AI technology's rapid development and expansion raises expectations for economic growth through this new technology:
- Modern AI (Artificial Intelligence) is defined as "algorithms capable of recognizing phenomena and reasoning like humans," distinct from robots or automation technology.
- Unlike traditional rule-based software, machine learning improves performance through data-based learning.
- Development of deep learning algorithms and natural language processing enables processing of diverse data types: images, text, and speech.
- The emergence and rapid development of generative AI has made AI a new form of production factor, recognized as an important driver of economic growth (Ministry of Industry and Trade, 2023).

**AI performance improvement speed**: Since 2010, AI performance has been improving 7 times faster than Moore's Law, doubling every 3.4 months (The AI Index 2019 Annual Report).

**AI's distinguishing features**: Unlike computers, software, and robots, AI can perform interpretation, classification, and creative tasks, and is expected to significantly affect a broader range of workers.

The major challenge: anticipate and respond agilely to AI-induced changes in labor demand to maximize competitiveness while minimizing negative impacts on job losses.

### 2. Research Objectives

Three research objectives:
1. **Empirically analyze** changes in employment by industry resulting from domestic firms' AI adoption
2. **Empirically analyze** changes in labor demand by industry and occupation based on occupational AI exposure indices derived from AI patent information and domestic industry AI technology influence
3. Based on empirical results and international digital talent policy analysis, **evaluate effectiveness of current talent development policies** and derive future policy tasks and improvements

## Chapter 2: Prior Research on AI's Employment Impact & Policy Trends

### 1. Prior Research Survey

#### (1) International Prior Research

**□ AI Exposure Index by Occupation**

**Felten et al. (2018)**:
- Analyzed AI's relative impact on occupations by linking AI application categories with occupational capabilities.
- Found total employment does not change due to AI, but affected occupations show wage increases → AI may deepen labor polarization.

**Webb (2019)**:
- Developed Exposure Index by linking AI patents with occupational task analysis — widely used as a proxy for labor substitution potential.
- Formula: Exposure Index = (sum of task-patent matching scores) / (total tasks)
- Found robots and software show employment decline as exposure increases, but AI's employment change was not clear.
- This index has been used in Acemoglu et al. (2022), Albanesi et al. (2023), and this study.

**□ AI's Labor Substitution Effect**

**Acemoglu et al. (2022)**:
- Analyzed AI's impact at firm and labor market levels using online job postings.
- At firm level: AI-related job postings surged; jobs unrelated to AI showed employment decline.
- At labor market level: No significant impact of AI found (despite industry and occupation-level analysis).

**Sousa & Sytsma (2023)**:
- Calculated exposure indices by 8 AI categories; found AI reduces employment, especially in occupations with high routine task content.
- In 2019, up to 15% of workers were employed in AI high-risk occupations. Higher-educated/higher-wage occupations show higher AI exposure.

**Eloundou et al. (2023) — GPT analysis**:
- LLMs will affect at least 10% of tasks for ~80% of workers, and at least 50% of tasks for ~19% of workers.
- Productivity gains expected; 15% to up to 56% of tasks could be performed faster by AI.

**□ AI's Performance Creation Effect**

**Alekseeva et al. (2021)**:
- AI skills demand surged dramatically over the past decade.
- Wage premium for AI-skill jobs: 11% within the same company, 5% versus the same job title.

**Babina et al. (2024) & Czarnitzki et al. (2023)**:
- AI investment surged across all sectors; AI-investing companies showed increased sales, employment, and market value through production innovation.
- Effect particularly concentrated in firms with high industrial monopoly power.

#### (2) Domestic Prior Research

**Han Jiwoo & Oh Samil (2023)**:
- Used Webb (2019) technology exposure indices to predict AI's role in reducing employment and suppressing wage growth rates.
- When AI exposure index increases 10 percentiles → employment predicted to fall 7%p, wage growth rate 2%p.

**Jeon Byungyoo et al. (2022)**:
- Calculated occupational AI exposure based on Felten et al. (2018) EFF.
- Found AI has employment increase effect and brings wage premium in high-education/high-wage occupations.
- Noted that AI may cause labor market polarization.

**Korea Research Institute for Vocational Education and Training**:
- Studies on how occupational tasks change with AI introduction and resulting occupational competency changes from workforce development perspective.
- Studies on automation risk assessment, skill change directions, and AI talent development system construction.

**Song Danbi et al. (2021)**:
- Observed AI utilization status and analyzed correlation between AI adoption and productivity in adopting firms.
- Using Enterprise Activity Survey: AI use did not lead to productivity increases (interpreted as AI still being in early adoption stage).

## Chapter 3: Analysis of AI Adoption's Employment Impact on Domestic Firms

### 1. AI Adoption Status of Domestic Firms (Enterprise Activity Survey)

**About the Enterprise Activity Survey**: Comprehensive annual survey of corporate management activities for domestic corporations with 50+ employees and capital of 300+ million won. Since 2017, has surveyed 9 fourth industrial revolution technologies including AI.

**AI Adoption Trends**:

| Year | Total surveyed | AI adopters | Adoption rate | Continuous users | Continuous rate |
|------|----------------|-------------|---------------|------------------|-----------------|
| 2017 | 12,579 | 173 | 1.41% | 51 | 0.41% |
| 2018 | 13,144 | 352 | 2.75% | 62 | 0.47% |
| 2019 | 13,255 | 407 | 3.15% | 57 | 0.43% |
| 2020 | 13,429 | 469 | 3.58% | 59 | 0.44% |
| 2021 | 13,448 | 504 | 3.85% | 157 | 1.17% |
| Total | 65,855 | 2,527 | 3.17% | 335 | 0.51% |

**AI Adoption by Industry** (top adopters):
1. J. Information and communication: 13.5%
2. K. Finance and insurance: 9.0%
3. P. Educational services: 6.7%

**Primary use of AI**: 65.4% for product development; also production processes (11.1%), marketing (9.6%), sales purposes (9.4%), organizational management (4.5%)

**Firm Characteristics of AI Adopters** (vs. non-adopters):
- Average employees: 1,672 vs. 318 (5.3× more)
- Revenue: 19,293 vs. 2,447 hundred million won (7.9× more)
- Firms with 1,000+ employees: 14.8% adoption rate vs. overall 3.0%
- Revenue 10+ trillion won: 35.9% adoption rate
- Affiliated with mutual investment-restricted corporate groups: 10.1% (4× non-affiliated firms)

### 2. Firm-Level Employment Impact Analysis (DID Model)

**Standard DID Model Results**:
- AI adoption is associated with firm size (AI adopters have 1.79–2.45× more workers), but no statistically significant employment change from adoption.
- Analysis by industry, revenue size, and with time lag all show no significant employment effect.
- Revenue and profit also show no significant improvement from AI adoption.

**Multi-Period DID Model (Callaway & Sant'Anna, 2021)**:
- Treatment group: firms that continuously used AI after initial adoption
- Control group: firms that never adopted AI

Without firm attribute controls:
- At adoption point: ~3.4% employment increase
- Post-adoption (1–3 years): ~4.3% increase on average
- Some statistical significance in G2019 and G2021 groups

With firm attribute controls (industry + revenue):
- Employment increase effect becomes insignificant
- No significant revenue increase effect

### 3. Implications

- AI adoption rate is extremely low at 2.9% of total firms; continuously adopting firms only 0.5%.
- AI-adopting firms have significantly larger employment and revenue scales.
- **DID analysis: No significant employment effect from AI adoption at firm level.**
- Revenue and profit improvements also not found — no expected employment inducement from future business performance improvement.
- Data limitations (short survey period 2017–2021, very few AI adopters) may have led to statistically insignificant results. Long-term large-scale data needed.

## Chapter 4: Analysis of AI's Impact on Labor Demand

### 1. AI Exposure Index (Webb 2019)

Webb (2019) developed the Exposure Index by:
1. Representing AI patent functions as verb-noun combinations
2. Representing occupational tasks as verb-noun combinations (using O*NET database)
3. Calculating similarity scores between patent tasks and occupational tasks
4. Weighting by task frequency and importance within each occupation

**Formula**:
```
Exposure(o,t) = Σ[w(k,o) × Σrf(c)] / Σ[w(k,o) × |{c : c ∈ S(k)}|]
```

Higher exposure index → higher potential for automation → higher labor substitution potential.

**Technology Comparison**:
- **Robots**: Highest exposure for machine operators, craft workers, elementary workers (routine manual tasks)
- **Software**: Highest for machine operators and clerical workers (routine cognitive tasks)
- **AI**: Relatively higher for managers and professionals — can replace occupations that robots and software cannot

### 2. At-Risk Jobs by Industry

Industries with highest proportion of AI-exposed jobs (75th percentile+):
1. Professional, scientific and technical services: 44.9%
2. Information and communication: 40.5%
3. Electricity, gas, steam: 41.3%

In absolute numbers: Manufacturing has the most AI-exposed jobs (26.2% of total).

### 3. Labor Demand Analysis Results

**Total Employment (2012–2022 changes)**:

| Variable | AI | Software | Robot |
|----------|-----|----------|-------|
| Exposure (basic) | −0.43%* | −0.79%*** | −0.90%*** |
| + Routine index | Not sig. | −0.52%** | −0.64%*** |
| + Non-routine index | −0.60%** | −0.73%*** | −0.81%*** |
| + R&D activity | Not sig. | −0.68%*** | −0.89%*** |

**Key Insight**: When controlling for task routineness, AI's employment reduction effect disappears, suggesting AI's apparent employment reduction was driven by routine tasks within AI-exposed occupations, not AI per se.

**Hiring Plans**:
- AI: Not significant (positive but insignificant)
- Robots: Significantly negative (−0.49%**)

**Job Openings by Skill Level** (2012–2022):
- AI total: +0.62%***
- AI – bachelor's/master's level: +1.17%***
- AI – doctoral level: +1.58%***
- Robot – doctoral level: −1.15%**

→ **AI-exposed occupations show increased demand for bachelor's–doctoral workers**, while robots reduce hiring across all skill levels.

**Task Routineness Analysis**:
- Routine task intensity reduces total employment by 17–28% per unit increase — stronger effect than technology exposure.
- Once routineness is controlled, AI's employment reduction disappears; software and robot effects remain.
- This suggests AI differs from software/robots in that its employment impact is mediated through routine task content.

**R&D Activity Analysis** (using industry AI patent applications as proxy):
- Industries with active AI R&D show significantly higher employment (+1.39% per patent unit).
- AI and software R&D are both concentrated in Information and Communication.
- AI requires doctoral-level high-skilled experts; software shows relatively more demand for bachelor's–master's level.

### 4. Conclusions

1. **AI most replaceable occupations**: Managers and professionals (especially construction/electrical managers, engineering/science professionals).
2. **Industry with highest AI-at-risk job ratio**: Professional, scientific services and Information and communication (40%+).
3. **AI's employment effect**: While overall employment declines with AI exposure, **bachelor's–doctoral hiring increases** — AI appears to complement high-skilled labor.
4. **AI vs. other technologies**: Unlike robots (routine manual tasks) and software (routine cognitive tasks), AI can replace non-routine cognitive tasks → broader range and higher-skilled occupations at risk.
5. **AI R&D employment effect**: Active AI R&D increases employment, especially high-skilled experts. Currently, AI R&D-induced job creation outweighs displacement effects.

## Chapter 5: AI Talent Development Status and Policy Tasks

### Summary of Key Analysis Results

| Analysis Results | Policy Implications |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| AI employment effects at firm and market level not yet visible | Preemptive response to AI job displacement needed |
| AI can replace managers and professionals — distinct from robots and SW | SW/AI talent differentiation and AI-specific policy needed |
| High demand for master's/doctoral high-skilled workers for AI development | Address barriers to foreign talent attraction |
| Domestic AI investment and talent development is government-led | Strengthen AI competitiveness through private-sector-led innovation |

### 1. SW·AI Talent Differentiation and AI-Specific Policy

**Problem**: Current digital talent development policies conflate AI and software without distinction.
- In the "4th Science & Technology Talent Development Plan (2021–2025)" 2023 action plan, 43% of AI-related sub-tasks use "SW·AI" jointly.

**Evidence for Different Skill Levels Needed**:
- Empirical analysis: AI-exposed occupations show significant increase in bachelor's–doctoral job openings; software's doctoral-level hiring not significantly affected.
- AI talent demand problem stems from **shortage of high-skilled R&D talent** (21,500 needed vs. only 4,900 domestic supply — shortage of 16,600: Ministry of Employment, 2023).

**Recommended Reforms**:

**Curriculum**:
- Method 1: Link SW-centered universities with AI graduate schools (or expand AI graduate school functions) to establish integrated bachelor's–master's–doctoral AI specialist training
- Method 2: Provide clear guidelines for mandatory AI-related curricula at undergraduate level to minimize inter-university curriculum gaps

**Evaluation**:
- Improve AI graduate school support program metrics to reflect excellence of trained talent (e.g., add "top conference paper submissions and presentations" metric alongside student satisfaction and enrollment counts)

### 2. Private-Sector-Led AI Innovation

**Current Situation**: Korea's AI competitiveness ranks 6th globally (Tortoise Intelligence), but government strategy (6th) far outpaces private investment (18th).
- 2013–2022 cumulative private investment: Korea $5.57B vs. US $248.9B vs. China $95.1B
- US: Private investment #1, government strategy #8 (opposite of Korea)

**US Big Tech Examples**:
- Amazon "AI Ready" project: Free AI skills training for 2 million people by 2025, $12M+ in scholarships
- Google: Tiered free education programs, Vertex AI machine learning platform for self-directed training
- Microsoft: AI Skills Initiative — free education and certification programs

**Recommendations**:
- **Short-term**: Encourage companies to run practical worker training (K-training, Innovation Academy) on their own; government focuses on basic academics (AI graduate schools)
- **Medium-long term**: Guide major corporations toward broader talent development similar to US big tech
- **Government role**: Provide incentives for private investment; focus direct investment on infrastructure and basic research rather than technology development

### 3. Proactive Foreign Talent Attraction

**International Comparison**:
- US: 82% of AI doctoral graduates chose US for education quality; 83% stayed after graduation
- China: Active R&D collaboration with foreign institutions; US-China collaborations increased 4× from 2010 to 2021
- India: Ranked 3rd in AI talent globally; abundant STEM talent

**Korea's Current Situation**:
- Only 2,551 AI experts — 0.5% of global AI talent (Global AI Talent Report 2020)
- Foreign talent exchange target: only 50 people in core technology fields
- Foreign startup visa period extension (1→2 years) has limited practical effect

**Recommendations**:
- Identify and remove barriers (language issues, compensation constraints, visa requirements)
- Proactively consider visa issue solutions for foreign talent attraction
- Develop active strategies similar to US and China models

### 4. Preemptive Response to AI Job Displacement

**Current Status**:
- AI's employment effects not yet visible (adoption rate ~3%, concentrated in large enterprises)
- But Goldman Sachs (2023) forecasts ~300 million full-time jobs will disappear globally

**Why AI Displacement Is Different**:
- **Robots**: Replace routine manual tasks (manufacturing, machine operators)
- **Software**: Replace routine cognitive tasks (clerical, administrative)
- **AI**: Can replace **non-routine cognitive tasks** (managers, professionals, legal, accounting) + amplify robot capabilities

→ Traditional solution of "upskilling to high-skilled jobs" has limitations because AI can also replace high-skilled jobs (Goldman Sachs estimates 50%+ job replacement in legal and administrative fields).

**Risk Assessment by Industry**:

| Technology | High-Risk Industries (40%+ exposure) | Low-Risk Industries |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|
| AI | Information/communication, Professional/scientific services | Wholesale/retail, Accommodation/food, Education, Healthcare |
| Software | Business facility management, Manufacturing | Education, Healthcare, Legal |
| Robot | Transportation (60%+), Manufacturing | Finance, Insurance, Professional services |

**Policy Recommendations**:

1. **Forecast jobs**: Identify occupations that will disappear and emerge, develop workers with required skills for new occupations.

2. **Value enhancement**: AI-resistant jobs (wholesale/retail, accommodation, healthcare) are mostly low-wage labor-intensive work → need measures to improve wages and occupational status.

3. **Scale employment in persistent jobs**: Consider ways to grow employment scale in jobs that will remain even after AI adoption.

4. **Productivity-employment linkage**: Technology adoption raises productivity → economic/industrial scale expansion → explore ways to expand (maintain) employment through productivity effects.

5. **Incentivize targeted adoption**: Productivity effects differ by industry and occupation → design incentives for AI adoption to start in sectors where productivity effects are maximized.
